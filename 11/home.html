The best musical songs

That you have to listen to!

Hamilton-You'll be back

This song is very inspiring, comedic yet chilling "breakup song" where King George III addresses the American colonists as a jilted, possessive lover. The 'lover' being England who Americans are breaking up with. And King George the third represent the 'jilted ex' who cannot deal that he has been broken up with. It is also very emotional and ranges in pitches and tones as he emotionally drifts in the mourning of the 'relationship'.

Heathers-Candy Store

This song represents the feeling of popularity and power in high school. It is used to persuade the lead (Veronica Sawyer) into choosing their path of cruel popularity opposed to her 'loser' friends. By saying 'candy' it is showing the outsider perspective of the popular lifestyle of glamourous, profiting benifits and how amazing it is to be one of them. While hiding the dark and manipulative reality.

Legally Blonde-Omigod you guys

This song follows the sorority sisters of Delta Nu at UCLA as they freak out over the rumour that Warner Huntington III is going to propose to Elle Woods that night. It has dramatic flair and shows Elle finding a dress, everything being perfect in the lead up to what they think is the 'proposal'. But it ends up being a breakupo between the two as Warner says she is just a dumb blonde and not good enough for him. Through the musical she ends up proving him wrong by getting into Harvard law and saying the well known line, 'What like it's hard?'

Good Morning Baltimore-Hairspray

This song is sang by the lead Tracy Turnblad sharing her optimism to the residents of Baltimore. It vis the opening introducing her and her character. The song reveals Tracy as a 'goddess of optimism' who sees beauty in everything around her, even the gritty or 'unsavory' parts of 1962 Baltimore. And the lyrics highlight her dreams of dancing on the Corny Collins show. With her exciting mood, it makes the audience sit on the edge of their seats in anticipation for what will happen next.

Six-Don't Lose Your Head

The phrase serves as a modern lens through which the musical reinterprets her historical narrative, shifting her from a 'villain' to a woman unapologetically navigating a dangerous court. However, the co-writer decided to put a playful spin on the topic of beheading one's wife and came up with this version.
