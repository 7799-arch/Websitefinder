Top 10 Places To Visit In Turkey

(in istanbul)

Kiz Kulesi*(the maiden's tower)

The Maiden’s Tower is an iconic lighthouse and former fortress perched on a tiny islet where the Bosphorus meets the Sea of Marmara. With a 2,500-year history, it has served as a customs station, a watchtower, and a legendary hideout for a Byzantine princess. Following a major 2023 restoration, this "floating" monument now serves as a museum and one of Istanbul’s most romantic sunset spots, accessible by a short boat ride from the Üsküdar shore.

Dolmabahçe Palace

The Dolmabahçe Palace is a 19th-century masterpiece on the Bosphorus that replaced the medieval Topkapi as the main residence for Ottoman Sultans. Built with a blend of Baroque, Rococo, and Neoclassical styles, it is famous for its extreme opulence, featuring 14 tonnes of gold leaf, a massive 4.5-tonne crystal chandelier, and the world's largest collection of Baccarat glassware. It also holds great national significance as the place where Mustafa Kemal Atatürk, the founder of modern Turkey, spent his final days.

İstiklal Avenue (İstiklal Caddesi)

İstiklal Avenue (İstiklal Caddesi) is the pulse of modern Istanbul—a vibrant, 1.4km pedestrian street that stretches from Taksim Square to the Galata district. Lined with grand 19th-century buildings, it is home to a mix of international brands, historic cinemas, hidden "passages" (arcades), and the iconic vintage red tram that rattles through its center. Visited by millions daily, it is the city's premier destination for shopping, street food, and nightlife, staying energetic well into the early hours of the morning.

Topkapi Palace

The Topkapi Palace was the opulent heart of the Ottoman Empire for nearly 400 years, serving as both the Sultan’s primary residence and the seat of government. Located on a hill overlooking the Bosphorus and the Golden Horn, the complex is a series of lush courtyards, ornate pavilions, and secretive chambers. Its most famous highlights include the Imperial Harem, the sacred relic rooms, and the Treasury, which houses the world-renowned 86-carat Spoonmaker’s Diamond and the emerald-encrusted Topkapi Dagger

Hagia Sophia

The Hagia Sophia is a 6th-century architectural masterpiece that has served as the world’s largest cathedral, an Ottoman mosque, a museum, and now once again an active mosque. Famous for its massive, "floating" dome and shimmering Byzantine mosaics, it represents the literal meeting point of East and West. While the ground floor is reserved for prayer, visitors can explore the upper galleries to see iconic Christian art and Viking runes carved into the marble, all set against a backdrop of grand Islamic calligraphy

Beylerbeyi Palace

The Beylerbeyi Palace is an elegant 19th-century summer residence for Ottoman Sultans located on the Asian shore of the Bosphorus, directly beneath the Bosphorus Bridge. Often described as a smaller, more intimate version of Dolmabahçe Palace, it was used to host visiting foreign heads of state, including Empress Eugénie of France and Shah Nasser al-Din of Persia. The palace is renowned for its Neo-Baroque exterior, cool marble interiors designed to provide a summer breeze, and a unique central hall featuring an indoor pool and fountain.

Theodosius Cistern (Şerefiye Sarnıcı)

The Theodosius Cistern (Şerefiye Sarnıcı) is a 1,600-year-old subterranean reservoir built by Byzantine Emperor Theodosius II to store water brought by the Valens Aqueduct. While smaller than the famous Basilica Cistern, it is arguably more impressive due to its meticulous restoration and 32 Corinithian marble columns wrapped in brass rings. Today, it functions as a high-tech art space, featuring a world-class 360-degree projection mapping show

Rumeli Hisari

Built by Sultan Mehmed the Conqueror in just four months, Rumeli Hisarı is a massive 15th-century fortress situated at the narrowest point of the Bosphorus. Constructed to cut off Byzantine supply lines before the conquest of Constantinople, its three Great Towers and sprawling curtain walls remain some of the most impressive military architecture in the world. Today, it serves as an open-air museum and concert venue

Adile Sultan Palace

The Adile Sultan Palace is an elegant 19th-century pavilion on Istanbul’s Asian side, originally built for the famous Ottoman poet Princess Adile Sultan. While it is a masterpiece of Neoclassical architecture and a premier event venue, it is most beloved as the filming location for the legendary Turkish film series Hababam Sınıfı. A dedicated museum room inside preserves the original movie classroom, making it a nostalgic pilgrimage site for fans of Turkish cinema.

Kuzguncuk

Kuzguncuk is a nostalgic, colourful "village" on the Asian side of the Bosphorus that feels like a peaceful time capsule. Famous for its well-preserved Ottoman wooden mansions and leafy streets, it is a rare historical example of religious harmony where churches, synagogues, and mosques sit side-by-side. Today, it is a favourite for locals and film crews alike, offering charming artisan bakeries, a communal vegetable garden (the Bostan), and a relaxed, bohemian atmosphere far from the city's tourist crowds

Galata Tower(kulesi)

Built in 1348, the Galata Tower is a 67-metre Genoese stone tower that dominates Istanbul’s European skyline. Once a medieval watchtower and prison, it now serves as a museum and observation deck offering 360-degree views of the Bosphorus and Golden Horn. Located in the trendy Beyoğlu district, it remains the city's most iconic spot for a sunset panorama.
