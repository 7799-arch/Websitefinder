One of us is lying

Top 10 themes

Secrets and Lies

The novel centers around hidden truths, as nearly every character has something they are trying to conceal, which becomes a major driving force for the plot. Simon’s gossip app threatens to expose the private secrets of other students, and his death becomes the climax of the consequences tied to those secrets. Bronwyn is hiding that she cheated on an exam, putting her academic honesty in question; Nate has a criminal past and is involved with drugs; Cooper conceals his sexuality to protect his reputation as a star athlete; and Addy struggles with loyalty to her boyfriend while questioning her own self-image. These lies increase tension throughout the story, mislead the investigation, and ultimately force the characters to confront both themselves and each other. Through this theme, the story encourages readers to question whether telling the truth is always freeing or whether secrecy can sometimes serve as a form of protection.

Identity and self-discovery

Many characters struggle between their true selves and the labels given to them at school. The murder investigation forces them to examine their actions and identities. Addy grows from a stereotypical “popular girl” into someone more independent, Cooper struggles with accepting his sexuality while maintaining his public image, and Bronwyn learns that perfection is unrealistic.

Peer pressure and social expectations

The novel shows how teenagers feel pressured to live up to certain roles. Bronwyn feels she must always be academically perfect, Cooper must maintain his reputation as a star athlete, and Addy believes her worth depends on popularity and her relationship. These pressures influence their decisions and behavior.

Bullying and scoical hierarchies

Simon controls this social power through his gossip app called About That. He posts students’ secrets online, which embarrasses them and can damage their reputation. Because people are afraid of their secrets being exposed, Simon has a lot of control over other students. After Simon dies, Bronwyn, Nate, Addy, and Cooper become the main suspects. Other students quickly start spreading rumors about them, even though there is no proof they are guilty. Many people believe the rumors because of the stereotypes they already have about the suspects. This shows that reputations in high school can be easily damaged by gossip and assumptions, and people are often judged before the truth is known.

Truth and perception

People in the story often judge others based on stereotypes rather than facts. Teachers, parents, and classmates assume the suspects are guilty because of their reputations. As the investigation progresses, readers realize that appearances can be misleading and the truth is more complicated.

Friendship and Trust

The four main suspects—Bronwyn, Nate, Addy, and Cooper—begin the story as students from completely different social groups. At first, they do not know each other well and even hold stereotypes about one another. For example, Bronwyn is seen as the “perfect student,” Nate as the “troublemaker,” Addy as the “popular girl,” and Cooper as the “star athlete.” These labels create distance between them and affect how they initially view each other. However, after they are all accused of being involved in Simon’s death, they are forced to spend time together and work as a group to figure out what really happened. Because they share the same situation and pressure from the police, media, and other students, they begin to understand each other better. As they talk about their secrets and personal struggles, they start to trust one another.Their friendship grows stronger throughout the story. They support each other emotionally and help each other deal with the stress of the investigation. For example, they defend each other when rumors spread and work together to uncover the truth. This friendship challenges the stereotypes that originally defined them. Instead of being just the “brain,” “criminal,” “princess,” or “athlete,” they begin to see each other as complex people with real problems and emotions. The novel shows that people are often more similar than they appear, and difficult situations can bring unexpected friendships.

Consequences of actions

The theme of consequences shows that every action, even small ones, can lead to serious results later. Many of the characters make choices that seem minor at first, but these decisions end up affecting their lives and the investigation.For example, Bronwyn secretly cheats on a chemistry test to maintain her perfect academic record. Although it may seem like a small mistake, it becomes a major problem when Simon plans to reveal it on his gossip app. If the truth comes out, it could damage her reputation and hurt her chances of getting into a top university. This shows how one dishonest decision can threaten her future.Nate is another example. Because he has previously sold drugs and has a criminal record, many people already see him as a “troublemaker.” When Simon dies, this reputation makes Nate one of the first people the police suspect. Even though he may not be responsible, his past actions make it harder for others to trust him.Simon’s own actions also lead to serious consequences. Through his gossip app, he constantly exposes students’ secrets and humiliates them publicly. While he believes he is simply revealing the truth, his behavior causes anger, resentment, and conflict among his classmates. This toxic environment contributes to the chain of events surrounding his death. Overall, the novel shows that choices have ripple effects. Actions can influence how people see you, affect your relationships, and sometimes lead to consequences far greater than expected.

Family and parental influence

Family life plays an important role in shaping how the characters think, behave, and deal with problems. Each of the main suspects comes from a different family environment, and these backgrounds influence their struggles and decisions.Nate grows up in a very unstable home. His mother left when he was younger, and his father struggles with alcoholism and cannot properly take care of him. Because Nate has had to look after himself for most of his life, he becomes independent but also distrustful of others. This difficult upbringing partly explains why he gets involved in illegal activities like selling drugs and why many people assume the worst about him. Cooper comes from a more stable family, but he faces strong pressure to succeed, especially in baseball. His father expects him to focus on his athletic future and maintain a perfect image. Because of this pressure, Cooper feels he must hide his true identity, particularly his sexuality, out of fear that it could disappoint his family or harm his career. This shows how family expectations can make it difficult for teenagers to be honest about who they are. Addy has parents who are often distracted by their own problems and do not pay close attention to her life. Because she lacks strong guidance and emotional support at home, she looks for approval from others at school. This is one reason she becomes so focused on popularity and her relationship with Jake at the beginning of the story. Over time, however, she begins to realize that she does not need to rely on others for her self-worth. Through these characters, the novel shows that family environments can strongly influence teenagers’ choices, confidence, and struggles, even if those influences are not always obvious.
